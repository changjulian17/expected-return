Methodology

Three ways to estimate expected returns — how they work, what they mean, and how to interpret the differences.

CAPM Total Return DCF IRR Spreads Putting It Together

1. CAPM + Country Risk Premium

What it measures: The return an investor should require to compensate for the risk of holding a stock, based on its exposure to market risk and country risk.

Formula

E(R) = Rf + βlevered × ERP + λ × CRP
VariableSourceWhat it is
Rf^TNX (Yahoo Finance)Current 10-year US Treasury yield — the baseline "risk-free" return available to any investor.
βleveredDamodaran industry betas × company D/EBottom-up levered beta. Starts with the unlevered beta for the company's industry (pure business risk), then relevers it for the company's debt level. A β of 1.0 means the stock moves with the market; >1 means more volatile.
ERPDamodaran implied ERPEquity Risk Premium — the extra return the stock market as a whole provides over risk-free bonds. Damodaran's "implied" ERP estimates this from current market pricing (currently ~4.8%).
λ (lambda)Company's revenue geographyShare of revenues exposed to a risky country. A company with 40% revenue in Brazil has λ=0.40.
CRPDamodaran country risk fileCountry Risk Premium — the additional equity risk from operating in a specific country, derived from sovereign default spreads and equity market volatility.

Derivation

  1. Get the risk-free rate from the 10Y US Treasury. This sets the baseline — the minimum return any investment should beat.
  2. Find the industry's unlevered beta from Damodaran's database of thousands of US-listed companies, grouped by industry. This isolates pure business risk from financing decisions.
  3. Relever it using the company's debt-to-equity ratio: βlevered = βunlevered × (1 + (1 − taxrate) × D/E). More debt = higher risk = higher required return.
  4. Multiply by ERP to convert beta into an expected excess return over the risk-free rate.
  5. Add CRP if the company has material non-US revenue in countries with sovereign risk.

What the number means

A CAPM of 8% means: "Given this stock's risk profile, you should expect at least 8% annualized return to justify owning it." A high CAPM suggests the stock is risky (high beta, high leverage, high country exposure). A low CAPM suggests defensive positioning or low leverage.

4–12% — Utilities and consumer staples cluster at the low end; tech, small caps, and emerging markets at the high end.

2. Total Return Decomposition

What it measures: The return an investor can expect from cash payouts and growth, independent of market pricing. It decomposes shareholder returns into three real sources.

Formula

E(R) = Dividend Yield + Buyback Yield + Expected Growth
ComponentSourceWhat it is
Dividend YieldYahoo Finance TTM dividends / Market CapCash dividends paid over the trailing twelve months as a percentage of market cap. Apple pays ~0.4%, no-dividend stocks like GOOGL get 0%.
Buyback YieldYahoo Finance shares outstanding change or cash spent on repurchases / Market CapThe reduction in share count from buybacks, expressed as a yield. A 2% share count reduction effectively returns 2% of value to shareholders. For most Mag 7 stocks, buybacks far exceed dividends.
Expected GrowthROE × Retention Ratio (or industry-based estimate)How fast the company can grow earnings by reinvesting profits. If a company earns 30% ROE and retains 60% of earnings, fundamental growth = 18%. Capped at 20% to avoid unrealistic projections.

Derivation

  1. Calculate dividend yield: Trailing 12-month dividends ÷ current market cap.
  2. Calculate buyback yield: Either the year-over-year decline in shares outstanding, or cash spent on buybacks divided by market cap, whichever is more reliable.
  3. Estimate growth: Start with ROE × retention ratio as the "fundamental" growth rate. If revenue data is available, blend it: fundamental growth and historical revenue growth are averaged (50/50). If only one is available, use it directly.
  4. Sum the three components to get the expected total return.

What the number means

Total Return tells you: "If the company keeps operating as it has been, what return can shareholders expect from the business itself?" It's independent of market price — it's about fundamentals.

A high Total Return (>15%) suggests the company has strong cash flows (dividends + buybacks) and/or high organic growth potential. A low or negative Total Return suggests a mature or declining business.

0–20% — Mature dividend payers at the low end; high-growth tech at the high end. Capped at ±25% for sanity.

3. DCF Implied IRR

What it measures: The return the market price is implying you'll earn if you buy today, given reasonable assumptions about the company's future cash flows.

Formula

EV = Σ FCFFt / (1 + r)t + TV / (1 + r)5
where r is the implied expected return (IRR)
ComponentSourceWhat it is
FCFFProjected from EBIT × (1−t) + D&A − Capex − ΔWCFree Cash Flow to the Firm — cash available to all capital providers (debt + equity) after reinvestment needs.
Revenue growth3-year historical CAGR or 5% defaultHow fast the top line grows. Capped at 40%, converged to terminal growth in later years.
Operating marginCompany current → Damodaran industry averageMargins converge linearly from the company's current margin to the industry average over 5 years. If the company already beats industry average, it stays there.
Sales/Capital ratioDamodaran industry dataHow much revenue each dollar of invested capital generates. Used to compute required reinvestment: more revenue growth needs more capital.
Terminal growth (g)Geography-adjusted (US: 2.5%, Brazil: 4.5%)Perpetual growth rate in the terminal value. Linked to the country's nominal GDP growth potential and risk-free rate.
WACCDamodaran industry WACCWeighted Average Cost of Capital — used as a discount rate reference, not to solve the IRR.

Derivation (how we solve for r)

  1. Project 5 years of FCFF. Revenue starts at the company's recent growth rate, gradually declining to the terminal growth rate. Margins converge to industry average.
  2. Calculate reinvestment as revenue growth × capital required (derived from the Sales/Capital ratio).
  3. Compute FCFF: EBIT after tax + depreciation − capex − change in working capital.
  4. Calculate terminal value: TV = FCFF5 × (1 + g) ÷ (r − g).
  5. Solve for r that makes the present value of all future FCFF + TV equal today's enterprise value. This is the implied IRR — the market's embedded return expectation.

What the number means

A DCF IRR of 6% means: "If you buy this company at today's price, and these cash flow projections are accurate, you'll earn about 6% per year."

High IRR (>10%): The market is pricing the stock cheaply relative to its cash flow potential. This could mean the stock is undervalued, or the market sees risks the DCF doesn't capture.

Low IRR (<5%): The stock is expensive relative to cash flows. The market is paying a premium, likely for growth or quality that the DCF understates.

N/A (not available): The company has negative or inconsistent FCFF projections (common for high-growth companies still investing heavily). The DCF model can't solve a meaningful IRR when cash flows are negative.

3–15% — Low for mega-cap growth stocks with high multiples; high for distressed or cyclical companies. TSLA often caps out at 50%.

4. Spreads: What the Differences Tell You

The real insight comes from comparing the three methods. Each spread isolates a different kind of mispricing or insight.

Spread 1: DCF − CAPM

DCF IRR − CAPM     # Market expectation vs. required risk premium

This spread compares what the market implies you'll earn (DCF) against what you should require for the risk (CAPM).

The market is pricing the stock to deliver returns above its cost of equity. This typically means the stock is cheap — the price is low enough that future cash flows are expected to yield a high return. Examples: cyclical downturns, temporary bad news creating a buying opportunity.
The market's implied return is below the risk-appropriate required return. The stock is expensive relative to its fundamentals. Investors are paying for growth or safety that the DCF assumptions don't fully capture. Examples: high-multiple growth stocks, bubble territory.

Typical range: −5% to +5%. Outside this range suggests extreme pricing — either a deep value opportunity or a significant overvaluation.

Spread 2: Total Return − DCF IRR

Total Return − DCF IRR     # Fundamental reality vs. market pricing

This compares what the business fundamentals say you should earn (dividends + buybacks + growth) against what the market price implies.

The company's real cash generation and growth potential exceed what the market is pricing in. The stock may be undervalued — the market hasn't fully appreciated the company's fundamental earning power.
The market expects more from the company than its current fundamentals can deliver. Either growth will accelerate (bull case) or the stock is overpriced. This is common for high-growth tech where the DCF captures conservative projections but the market is pricing in a more optimistic scenario.

Typical range: −8% to +8%. High-growth stocks tend to have negative spreads here because the market prices in future margin expansion that the DCF gradually converges to.

Spread 3: Total Return − CAPM

Total Return − CAPM     # Fundamental return vs. risk requirement

This compares what the business delivers from operations against what investors should require as compensation for risk. It's the most "independent" measure — neither number depends on market price.

The company's fundamental return exceeds its cost of equity. The business is creating value — its operations earn more than investors require. This is the hallmark of a company with a durable competitive advantage (economic moat).
The company doesn't earn enough from operations to compensate for the risk. Investors would be better off in risk-free bonds. This can happen in distressed industries, turnarounds, or businesses with poor capital allocation.

Typical range: −5% to +10%. Consistently positive spread is a sign of quality — think of it as the company's "economic profit margin."

Putting It All Together

DCF IRR > CAPM (cheap) + TR > DCF (fundamentals strong) + TR > CAPM (value creation).
The stock is cheap, its fundamentals are strong, and it earns above its cost of equity. This is the ideal combination — deep value with quality.
DCF IRR < CAPM (expensive) + TR < DCF (market expecting too much) + TR < CAPM (value destruction).
The stock is expensive, the market expects growth the company can't deliver, and the business doesn't earn its cost of equity. Time to be cautious.

If DCF−CAPM is green but TR−DCF is red: The stock looks cheap, but the market is still pricing in growth the fundamentals don't support. Possible value trap.

If DCF−CAPM is red but TR−CAPM is green: The stock is expensive, but the underlying business is high quality and creates real value. Premium may be justified.

If TR−DCF is green but TR−CAPM is negative: Fundamentals are decent and the stock is cheap, but the business doesn't earn enough to justify its risk. Cyclical or distressed.

Limitations

  • CAPM relies on a single factor (beta). Real returns are driven by multiple risk factors (value, size, momentum, profitability).
  • Total Return is backward-looking — it uses past dividend/buyback/growth data that may not persist.
  • DCF IRR is only as good as its assumptions. Growth rates, margins, and terminal value are estimates that can be wrong.
  • Spreads don't mean "buy" or "sell." They're diagnostic tools — a large negative DCF−CAPM spread for a high-growth company is normal and expected.
  • The models assume everything is denominated in USD. Currency risk, inflation, and regulatory changes are not explicitly captured.
